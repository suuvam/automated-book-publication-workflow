"The Gates of Morning"

CHAPTER I

THE CANOE BUILDER

D
ICK, standing on a coral ledge, cast his gaze to the South.

The breakers of the outer sea roared behind him, scattering spindrift on the wind; before him lay the lagoon of Karolin, as calm as a lake, seemingly infinite, blue, and dotted with fishing gulls—an ocean enclosed by a 40-mile ring of coral.

This vast pond, a sea in heavy winds and a lake of azure in light airs, was his—he who had only arrived the previous day.

As Dick looked towards the canoe houses, his brow furrowed, and he turned his back to the lagoon, facing the breakers on the outer beach and the northern sea. Beyond the sea line, invisible, lay Palm Tree, an island as beautiful as a dream yet infested with malevolent beings.

Little Tari, the net maker's son, inquired, "Taori, who are you?" (é kamina tai)

Dick found it challenging to explain his past to the child, for his memories before meeting Katafa on Marua, another island, had grown blurry. He had lived there with an old sailor named Kearney, who had taught him to fish, spearfish, and build toy ships. Recalling these events, Dick realized how much he owed to Kearney for his survival and education.

He looked down at Tari and replied, "I am Taori, Tari tatu, why do you ask?"
